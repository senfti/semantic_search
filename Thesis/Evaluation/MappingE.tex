
\section{Mapping}

The quality of the maps are investigated after a complete exploration of the environment without object search. This was done in all three environments multiple times. In this section typical results of those exploration runs are presented and also encountered problems. At first the metric maps are investigated, followed by the Room-Type-Maps and the Object-Probability-Maps. At the end of this section the estimations after peeking into a room are presented.

The 2D maps of the rooms have in general a good quality, at least those of small rooms. All maps contain cells wrongly set to free outside of the room because of the handling of invalid range measurements described in section \ref{preprocessing}. However those cause no major problems. In larger rooms the quality of the maps decreases. In the map of room lab1 the top part leans to the left while the bottom right part is rotated towards the bottom left part. In the map of the room institute1 the bottom corridor is curved upwards. The main reason for these distortions is probability the limited range of the LIDAR, which results in many invalid range measurements in open areas. In further consequence less distinctive features are seen by the LIDAR, so the localization of the robot has to rely on the odometry, which can lead to inaccurate localization and therefore also inaccurate maps. The larger the rooms are the more impact those inaccuracies have. This shows the benefit of the hybrid map design, which splits larger environments into easier to handle room maps. 

As can be seen in the hybrid maps the doorway mapping works nicely, both the positions and orientations of the doorway poses are accurate in almost all cases. The only exception is the doorway between the rooms lab1 and lab3, where a door closer caused the shift to the left. But as the doorway pose is still within the doorway and the shift is the same in both rooms, this causes no problems.

Assigning a ground truth Room-Type-Map is not straight forward because for some areas multiple room types are fitting and the boundary between areas of different room types is often not sharp. Therefore images, shown in Figure \ref{wz_imgs}, are given together with a map of the corresponding view poses, shown in Figure \ref{wz_views}. From the images one can see that the left is a kitchen area while the bottom right is a living room area. Between those areas is a dining table. The area with the TV can be classified as living room, but the rest of the room cannot really be classified. These areas are also depicted in Figure \ref{wz_views}.

In image b of Figure \ref{roomclass} the Room-Type-Map created without the usage of the correlation between room types described at the end of section \ref{Room-Type-Map} is shown. On the one hand the dining room area is much larger there. This is caused by the uncertainty of the room type classification between the similar room types kitchen and kitchenette. Without considering this similarity a third, different room type becomes more likely. On the other hand the closet area is much larger because it is not penalized for not really fitting to the other room types. In general the impact of using the correlation between room types is not that big, but the tests showed at least a small positive effect.

In the images c and d of Figure \ref{roomclass} the resulting Room-Type-Maps after other runs are shown. After run 2 the living room area is much larger than after run 1, whereas in run 3 the kitchen and kitchenette area is much larger. This shows the impact of the trajectory during the exploration on the result. The trajectory decides which areas are seen how often and from which direction and which areas are seen together in one image. Especially the room type of areas, where the room type classifier is undecided, depends highly on the trajectory. Also the border between areas of different room type is shifted depending on the taken images because one room type might be overruled by the neighboring room type, as described in section \ref{vision_eval}.

In image e of Figure \ref{roomclass} the result after an object search is shown. Here the favor of the room type classification to detect kitchenettes can be seen. Also noteworthy is the nice shape of the lining room area and the basement area, which is probability cause by the robot looking into the wall.

\subsection{Object Probability Map}

This section evaluates the Object-Probability-Maps after the exploration of the room home1. This evaluation is done using the 2D projections of the Object-Probability-Maps for better clarity and because the 2D projection is used in the search planning. The Object-Probability-Map is build from two sources, the Object-Map containing seen objects and the object probabilities calculated from the Room-Type-Maps. 

In Figure \ref{objfound} all cells with a probability greater than 0.25 in any Object-Probability-Map are depicted together with the type of the object. As in some cells multiple objects were seen, this is visualized in three images to have no overlap. In the results of both runs can be seen that big objects, like sofas or tables, are well detected. However, the object probabilities of those objects is also high in the areas around the objects due to the missing per-pixel segmentation of the objects. For example also parts of the dining table have a high probability for being a sofa. For the search planning this is usually no problem as the object is either already found during the exploration or found quickly anyway. On the other hand only some smaller objects have a high probability in the Object-Probability-Map. Small objects are less often detected and so the object probability does not increase high enough. The seen objects depend on the trajectory of the robot. For example the monitor was not seen in run 1 or the cups were seen at different locations. Especially small objects are seen in some runs and not in other runs as their detection does not work as well and it is more likely that they are not seen often enough.

These probabilities depend on the Room-Type-Maps, which can be seen nicely in the images. For example a fork is more likely in a dine room than in areas of other room type or a dog more likely in a living room. On the other hand a suitcase is very unlikely in a kitchen. \subsection{Probability Estimations after Peek Task}

For the system to work well the estimation of the probability for a searched object being in a room after the peek task is very important because based on this estimate the robot decides if the room should be investigated further or if other rooms are more promising. As only a fraction of the room is seen during the peek task, the estimation of object probability depends mostly on the estimation of the rest of the room.
